    Abstract

    Building performance research using various informatics techniques has progressed extensively in the last twenty years by advancing the fields of automated fault detection and diagnostics (AFDD), commissioning, data mining, and visualization for commercial buildings. Despite this effort, it has been difficult to understand the effectiveness of different approaches as compared to each other as there is a lack of general, public benchmarking datasets for this industry. We propose a repository in which researchers can release their detailed raw datasets for the purpose of repeatability, benchmarking, and utilization by other researchers. We start this effort through the public release of a single, large building performance seed dataset. The dataset is from a primary and secondary school campus that has 76,000 square meters floor area of conditioned, indoor space in seven buildings that include classroom, office, sports facilities, auditorium, cafeteria, dormitory, and mixed-use spaces. The dataset contains almost 3 years of detailed temporal data from 3,690 measured data points, most of which are sampled at a frequency of 1-3 minutes. The campus is located in a tropical climate with a continuously high cooling and dehumidification load. Some of the dataset has been annotated with building event schedules and known anomalous behavior which can be used as ground truth for detection algorithms. The dataset is available for download online and will serve as the first example in a planned repository of raw datasets from various buildings from different climates and contexts.

    1 Introduction

    Measured sensor data in commercial buildings has exploded in quantity and quality since the introduction of digital automation and management systems (Khan 2011). Various researchers have addressed the need to analyze and interpret this data into meaningful information to take action in improving the performance of the building systems and, therefore, the comfort, safety and satisfaction of the occupants in the building. However, many of these efforts are done on individual, isolated case studies, thus restricting the ability to understand the scalability of such techniques. The building industry contains various technical and practical barriers to accessing, cleaning, and utilizing large measured datasets from commercial buildings. These barriers create an environment in which researchers spend a large percentage of valuable time convincing operators to share their data and, if successful, collecting and cleaning data from building management systems that are not often designed as data acquisition and management systems.

    These difficulties leads to protectionist attitudes on the part of researchers of their data due to the investment needed to collect the data from commercial buildings. It is accepted by the data science community that this type of behavior leads to the phenomenon of implementation bias and data bias  (Keogh 2003). In building performance informatics, these biases usually manifest themselves as neglect of even comparing a competing technique or utilizing a benchmark dataset at all. In order to facilitate more verifiable research, we propose the development of benchmarking datasets for sub-hourly temporal measured datasets that are most useful in energy informatics research. This type of data is different from other public datasets which generally provide characteristic data of buildings (ie: use type, year built, system types, etc.) and sometimes monthly high-level electrical consumption. We start this effort through the release of a large, commercial building dataset with detailed data and a certain amount of annotated scheduling information. The rest of this paper will outline the details of this dataset, how and where to retrieve it, and what the future plans are for a larger data repository. We draw inspiration from the projects recently announced in the fields of residential building non-intrusive load monitoring and disaggregation research (Makonin 2013, Anderson 2012).

    The dataset was collected from conventional a building management system with integrated data collection and management platforms. A general overview of the types of data available in the dataset can be found in Figure 2. This diagram, in combination with a general understanding of the spatial layout of the campus, will assist a user in navigation of the dataset. Included in the dataset are detailed measured data from 105 electrical sub-meters, 633 air-side system, 414 water-side system, and 385 lighting points. The campus has 5,630 kW (1600 RT) of cooling system capacity from three conventional electrical chillers and 1500 kW (425 RT) from a solar thermal-powered absorption chiller. A log of various school schedules and known anomalies, faulty and non-faulty, is included for a subset of the data.
